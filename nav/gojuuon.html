the 50 japanese sound table**, not 50 sounds in total, but 50 spaces on table as of modern-day... 50 'SOUNDS' + N, blank spaces, archaic characters/not in common use, 45+1(n) in use, kana designed after certain kanji, ***radicals

click each line of kana to practice writing each sound in both systems

If you're having trouble associating a character with its sound, consider checking out the mnemonics page.

where you will find existing charts depicting picture mnemonics for both hiragana and katakana, although if you are feeling particularly creative,

feel free to come up with your own. Try using notecards or rectangular cut-outs of printer paper to make flashcards! If you don't have access to those,

some great software for making and using flashcards include anki.com, quizlet.com, memrise.com, and so on.

you MUST practice writing. but reading takes repeated exposure. the way i learned to read kana was by trying to read kana sentences with furigana, trying to guess before looking; and by transcribing(?) kana to romaji and vice versa. If you choose this strategy, you will have to learn some Japanese before you can read kana fluently. I believe this is easier than taking the characters as simply as pictographs and trying to attach a sound to it. Similar rules will definitely apply to kanji learning in the future. Learning to decipher Japanese text can be likened to piecing together a puzzle made of components, made of components.
