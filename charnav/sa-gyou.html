The first line of Japanese kana also constitute the 5 Japanese vowels. In order, these are: "a" (ah), "i" (ee), "u" (oo), "e" (eh), and "o" (oh).

Generally, when learning a foreign language, especially one with a different script to your native tongue (i.e. English --> Japanese), it is best practice to physically

write down new characters and words. This helps immensely with retention by building muscle memory as you learn to recognize and comprehend the language without

the help of romanization.

Remember those tracing worksheets you were given as a kid when learning how to write letters? They're back and fancier than ever! As non-native speakers,

we will frequently be going back to the basics.

Examine the following GIFs, and notice the stroke order. Then, practice writing these characters in the canvases, bearing in mind the sound they each represent.

If you wish to reset the canvases, simply refresh the page. Alternatively, you can use a piece of paper and a writing utensil.

We are not looking for perfection -- calligraphy is a different skill entirely; right now, we are simply aiming for legibility and reproducability.

This is to say, as long as you or I or a native Japanese speaker can easily determine the character you are trying to write, you have achieved the ability to write it.
